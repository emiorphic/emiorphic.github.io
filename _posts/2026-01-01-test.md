It is actually possible to derive conservation of mechanical energy in a rather elementary way. We need only three axioms and basic calculus. 

# Setup
Consider a system of $$N$$ particles, indexed by $$i$$, with masses $$m_i$$. We assume the following three axioms:
1. **Newton's Second Law:**

    $$
    m_i \vb{a}_i = \sum_{j=1, j \neq i}^{N} \vb{F}_{ij}.
    $$
2. **Newton's Third Law:**

    $$
    \vb{F}_{ij} = -\vb{F}_{ji}.
    $$
3. **Conservative Central Forces:**

    $$
    \vb{F}_{ij} = -u'(r_{ij}) \vu{r}_{ij}
    $$
    
    where $$r_{ij} = \norm{\vb{r}_{ij}}$$ and $$u(r_{ij})$$ is the pair potential.

Next, we define the total mechanical energy $$E = T + U$$ of the system as the sum of kinetic and potential energy $$T$$ and $$U$$, where

$$
T = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{2} m_i (\vb{v}_i \dotproduct \vb{v}_i) \quad \text{and} \quad U = \sum_{i \leq i < j \leq N} u(r_{ij}).
$$

Our goal is to prove that the quantity $$E$$ is conserved, i.e.

$$
\dv{E}{t} = 0.
$$

# Derivation
We differentiate $$T$$ with respect to $$t$$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \dv{T}{t} &= \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{2} m_i \dv{t} (\vb{v}_i \dotproduct \vb{v}_i)\\
    &= \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i (\vb{v}_i \dotproduct \vb{a}_i).
\end{aligned}
$$
